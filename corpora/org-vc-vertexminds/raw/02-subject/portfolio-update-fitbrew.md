# Portfolio Update: FitBrew

**Date:** November 15, 2025
**Analyst:** Tomás Herrera
**For:** Daniel Reeves, Ava Lindström
**Investment Status:** WARNING

---

## Quick Snapshot

**Company:** FitBrew
**Thesis:** Direct-to-consumer (D2C) personalized functional supplements for amateur athletes
**Investment:** €130K for 11% equity (3 months ago, August 2025)
**Current Valuation:** ~€1.18M pre-money (implied)
**Status:** Deteriorating unit economics; elevated burn relative to plan

---

## Current Metrics (October 2025)

| Metric | October Value | August Value | Variance | Plan (Oct) |
|--------|---|---|---|---|
| MRR | €4,200 | €3,100 | +35% | €5,800 |
| Active Subscribers | 180 | 110 | +64% | 240 |
| CAC | €45 | €28 | +60% | €22 |
| LTV (90-day projection) | €135 | €155 | -13% | €175 |
| Monthly Churn | 8.2% | 6.0% | -2.2pp | 4.5% |

**Translation:** FitBrew is growing subscriber count and MRR, but deteriorating on all efficiency metrics. This is a classic red flag pattern.

---

## What Went Wrong

### CAC Spike

Customer acquisition cost increased from €28 to €45 (+60%) between August and October. Founder Elena attributed this primarily to:
- **Meta CPM inflation:** Facebook and Instagram advertising costs rose significantly in Q4 (typical seasonal pattern). Average CPM for health/fitness vertical increased ~35% YoY in September–October per Meta's own reporting.
- **Ad creative fatigue:** The founding team had been running the same core creative (before/after transformation photos, athlete testimonials) since launch. By August, CTR had declined 22%, forcing increased bid prices to maintain conversion volume.

**Our Assessment:** The Meta CPM excuse is partially valid—Q4 is always expensive—but the creative fatigue suggests inadequate testing discipline. A world-class D2C founder would have 4–6 new creative variants in rotation by month 3. FitBrew's CEO, Elena, is a former fitness influencer, not a marketer. This is showing.

### Churn Acceleration

Monthly churn increased from 6.0% to 8.2%. At 8.2% monthly churn, annualized customer retention is just 38%—meaning the cohort of customers acquired in August will retain only 38% by August 2026.

**Qualitative feedback from churned customers:**
- "Personalization promise didn't match reality" (3 responses)
- "Formula didn't deliver noticeable benefits" (2 responses)
- "Price increase surprised me" (1 response; FitBrew raised subscription from €35 to €41/month in September)

The product is a quiz-based personalization engine that recommends supplement stacks (protein, creatine, vitamins, etc.). Early users (August–September cohort) were enthusiasts; later cohorts appear to be more price-sensitive and skeptical of perceived "personalization" (which is largely template-based, per our technical audit).

### Burn Rate Outpacing Plan

The burn rate increased from €13K to €18K/month (+38%). This is driven by:
- Increased ad spend (natural consequence of higher CAC bids)
- Team expansion (Elena hired a part-time customer success contractor in September, €3K/month)
- Inventory buildup (fulfillment partner Flexport required 60-day prepayment for Q4 inventory, adding €8K working capital pressure)

The original plan projected €10.5K/month burn through December. At €18K/month, cash runway has collapsed from a planned 8+ months to ~5.8 months.

---

## Root Cause Analysis: The D2C Economics Lesson

FitBrew reinforces what we suspected about D2C economics: **founder burn rate projections are systematically optimistic.**

We've observed this pattern across D2C companies we've tracked:

1. **Founder assumes 2–3% churn.** Reality: 6–8% is the norm in fitness/supplements (high impulse category, low switching costs).
2. **Founder assumes stable CAC.** Reality: Q4 is expensive; creative fatigue is inevitable; CAC drifts upward 40–60% within 6 months for most D2C players.
3. **Founder assumes "virality" or organic growth.** Reality: D2C paid social is the primary channel, and it's capital-intensive.
4. **Founder underestimates working capital.** Reality: Inventory, fulfillment partner prepayments, and reverse logistics drain cash faster than profit-and-loss suggests.

FitBrew's **correlation with Meta CPM increases is real**, but this is a structural risk for any D2C brand relying on paid social. We should have flagged this more explicitly during diligence.

## Current Situation

**Positive Factors:**
- MRR is growing (35% in 3 months). The company is acquiring customers, not contracting.
- The founding team is responsive and aware of problems. Elena has accepted the metrics are bad and is exploring alternatives (e.g., fitness influencer partnerships, affiliate programs).
- Gross margins are healthy at ~50% (COGS + fulfillment ~€21 per €41 subscription).

**Negative Factors:**
- Runway is now ~5.8 months. The company will need additional capital by April 2026 or face severe constraints.
- Unit economics are deteriorating, not improving. LTV:CAC ratio is now 3.0:1 (was ~5.5:1 in August); healthy D2C is typically 3.0+ but trending in the wrong direction.
- The founder team lacks D2C depth. Elena (CEO) is a fitness influencer; co-founder Ravi (COO) is an ex-supplement salesman. Neither has scaled a D2C brand or managed paid acquisition systematically.
- The product positioning ("personalization") is not defensible. There is no IP; the quiz logic could be replicated within weeks by a competent team.

## Risks

1. **Inability to Raise Series A:** With 5.8 months of runway and deteriorating unit economics, external investors will view FitBrew as distressed. Series A terms (valuation, dilution) will be significantly worse than August when we invested.

2. **Operational Pivot Required:** Elena and Ravi may need to shift strategy (e.g., B2B2C via fitness studios, focus on high-LTV athlete cohorts, reduce paid social exposure). A pivot requires time and capital neither is abundant.

3. **Founder Morale:** The gap between plan and reality is widening. We should watch for founder fatigue or key personnel departures.

---

## Questions for the Founders

1. **CAC Outlook:** What is the plan to stabilize CAC by Q1 2026? Specific creative testing roadmap and new channel exploration (TikTok, YouTube, affiliate)?

2. **Churn:** What cohort analysis shows about retention? Are August cohorts (early adopters) retaining better than October cohorts (mainstream)?

3. **Runway & Series A:** What is the realistic timeline for Series A fundraising? Do they have relationships with seed-stage D2C VCs (e.g., Kindred Ventures, Lerer Hippeau)?

4. **Pivot Optionality:** Have they explored B2B2C channels (selling through fitness studios, personal trainers, gyms)? This would reduce reliance on paid social and potentially improve LTV.

5. **Inventory Adjustment:** Can they reduce prepayment commitments with Flexport or diversify fulfillment partners to improve cash burn?

---

## Recommendation

**HOLD with Active Monitoring**

We will **not** make a follow-on investment at this time, given:
- Deteriorating unit economics
- Elevated burn relative to plan
- Founder inexperience in D2C scaling

However, we will:

1. **Request monthly updates** from Elena through March 2026 (runway threshold).
2. **Convene an IC call** in early 2026 to reassess if metrics stabilize.
3. **Facilitate Series A conversations** if Elena is actively fundraising, but expect unfavorable terms.

If metrics stabilize (CAC back to €35, churn below 6%) within 60 days, we will reconsider follow-on conviction. If metrics continue to deteriorate, we should be transparent with Elena that we view this as a founder/execution issue, not a market issue.

---

## Broader Takeaway for IC

D2C businesses are capital-efficient at small scale but become capital-intensive as paid social CPMs rise and organic channels saturate. Founder experience in paid performance marketing is a critical diligence factor we should weight more heavily going forward.

For early-stage D2C, we should:
- Demand historical CAC curves and cohort retention analysis (not just MRR growth).
- Stress-test assumptions against realistic paid social CPM trends.
- Verify that founding teams have either (a) prior D2C scaling experience or (b) strong fractional CMO support.

FitBrew did not meet these criteria; we took on execution risk we may not have fully appreciated.
