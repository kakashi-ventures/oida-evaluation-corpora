## MARCUS PRESENTS MERIDIAN LABS METHODOLOGY (10:12–10:27)

[10:12] Marcus: So, as you know, Meridian Labs specializes in operational process review and organizational transformation. We've had the opportunity to work with several firms in the compliance, documentation, and financial services space, and we're genuinely excited to bring that experience to ClearPath. But let me walk you through our methodology, because I think this matters—how we actually work is as important as what we deliver.

[10:14] Marcus: Here's how we think about this engagement. We believe that most organizations operate in two realities simultaneously. There's the formal reality—the org charts, the documented procedures, the policy manuals. And then there's the actual reality—how people really work day to day, what they really prioritize, where the informal networks actually make decisions. Our job is to map both of those realities and understand not just where they diverge, but why they diverge. Because that gap? That's where you'll find your biggest opportunities.

[10:16] Marcus: We do this through structured observation, which sounds fancy but is actually pretty straightforward. We embed ourselves in your operations. We sit with teams, we watch workflows, we take notes on what actually happens versus what the procedures say should happen. We're not auditors—we're not looking for violations. We're anthropologists, in a way. We're trying to understand the logic of how things actually work.

[10:18] Marcus: The second pillar is interviews. We talk to people at every level—frontline staff, middle management, leadership. And we use a methodology called triangulation, which means we ask the same questions in different ways to different people and look for patterns. If five people tell us the same thing independently, we know it's real. If everyone tells us something different, we know there's either confusion or misalignment, which is itself very informative.

[10:20] Marcus: The third piece is document analysis and systems review. We look at what people are actually using to do their work—your processes, your templates, your systems, your outputs. We trace work as it flows through the organization. All of this together gives us a 360-degree view.

[10:22] Marcus: As for timeline, we structure this in three distinct phases, each with clear deliverables. Phase One is discovery—roughly two to three weeks. We're here, we're embedded, we're collecting data through observation and interviews. Phase Two is analysis and synthesis—about two to three weeks. We take everything we've gathered and we create a comprehensive diagnostic picture. What's working, what's not, where are the bottlenecks, where are the redundancies, where are the quick wins.

[10:24] Marcus: Phase Three is recommendations and roadmap. We give you prioritized actions—short-term wins you can hit in 30 days, medium-term structural changes, longer-term transformations if necessary. Critically, we also map dependencies. What has to happen first? What's contingent on what? And we quantify resource implications as much as we can—how many people, what skill sets, what timeline. We're not giving you pie-in-the-sky recommendations. Everything is grounded in reality.

[10:26] Marcus: The deliverables vary slightly by client, but typically you get a written diagnostic report—usually 40 to 60 pages, which sounds like a lot but isn't once you have process maps, flowcharts, data tables. You get an executive summary for board-level communication. And we usually do a series of presentations—one for senior leadership, one for teams, one for the board if that's relevant. The goal is that everyone understands not just what we found, but why it matters and what we're asking them to do about it.

[10:27] Marcus: Questions on that so far?

## THOMAS ASKS SUBSTANTIVE QUESTIONS (10:27–10:35)

[10:28] Thomas: Yes, actually. Three things. First—how do you handle it when teams are resistant to being observed? Because I'll be honest, I know some people on my team, and the moment they know someone's watching their workflow, they're going to behave differently. So how do you actually get authentic data when people are, you know, on their best behavior?

[10:30] Marcus: That's a great question, and it's real. We account for it in a few ways. First, we build in observation time. People normalize our presence relatively quickly—usually in about a week or so, they stop performing. We're just... there. We become part of the background. Second, we don't announce that we're doing detailed workflow observation in the first week. We do a fairly soft introduction—"Marcus and I will be around, we'll be talking to people, we're trying to understand how ClearPath works." By the time people realize we're documenting their actual workflows, they've already stopped caring because it's become routine. Third—and this is important—we're very transparent that we don't care about individual performance. We tell people explicitly: we're not evaluating you, we're not reporting to anyone about what *you* specifically do. We're looking for system-level patterns. That actually disarms a lot of the resistance.

[10:33] Thomas: *nods slowly* Okay, that makes sense.

[10:34] Marcus: And frankly, in my experience, the initial resistance usually isn't about people being difficult. It's about fear of the unknown. Once they understand that we're not here to get them in trouble, and once they see that our observations actually lead to changes that make their work easier, they tend to become allies. Some of our best insights come from people who were skeptical at first.

[10:35] Thomas: Fair enough. Second question—what happens if we discover that the problem isn't process? What if we discover that the problem is people? Like, what if I have someone in a critical role who's just... not performing? Do I find that out in your report?

[10:37] Marcus: *thoughtful pause* That's a delicate one. Here's my honest answer: if there's a systemic people problem—meaning the role itself is poorly designed, or the person is in a role that doesn't fit their skills, or they don't have the support they need—we'll flag that. We might say something like, "This role needs clearer authority, or better training, or different reporting structure." What we don't do is provide personal performance evaluations. That's not our job, and frankly, it's dangerous territory. However—and this is important—if we observe that someone is actively undermining process or creating blockages, and it's clear, then yes, we'll flag it. We'll say it as professionally as we can, but we'll say it.

[10:39] Thomas: But not like, "Thomas, you need to fire David," kind of thing.

[10:40] Thomas: *laughs* I'm just checking the parameters here.

[10:40] Marcus: *smiles* Exactly. We might say, "The CTO role needs more structured time for strategic planning," or "There's a gap in technical documentation that's creating bottlenecks." But we're not HR consultants. We're not performance coaches. You understand?
