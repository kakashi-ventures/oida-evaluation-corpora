# Demo Day Pitch: GreenLoop
**Date:** November 12, 2025
**Time:** 17:48–18:08 CET
**Location:** Palazzo delle Industrie, Milan
**Participants:** Luca Ferretti (GreenLoop CEO), Stefan Berger (GreenLoop COO), Daniel Reeves (IC), Ava Lindström (IC), Tomás Herrera (Analyst)

---

[17:48] Daniel: Alright, next up we have GreenLoop. Luca, Stefan, welcome.

[17:50] [scattered applause as founders take their seats]

[17:51] Luca: Thank you. Hi, I'm Luca Ferretti, CEO of GreenLoop. This is Stefan Berger, our COO. Um, so we're solving a really concrete problem in the construction industry. Every day, contractors finish projects and they've got leftover materials. Concrete, steel, wood, equipment. They need to get rid of it. And right now they're either, um, sending it to landfill or they're doing these inefficient, local handshake deals with other contractors.

[18:07] Luca: We built a B2B marketplace that connects contractors with surplus materials to contractors who need them. Think of it like a, um, like an eBay for construction excess.

[18:15] Luca: [clicks to traction slide] So far we've got 45 companies on the platform. We've done €180K in GMV over the last six months. That's, um, that's real transactions. And we're growing month-over-month.

[18:24] Luca: The way we make money is we take a 9% take rate on each transaction. So our revenue is, um, roughly €16K so far. The margins are good because the platform is basically a digital marketplace — low marginal cost once we've onboarded the sellers.

[18:36] Luca: [clicks to operational slide] What makes us special is our last-mile optimization. So it's not enough to match a buyer and seller. We also help coordinate pickup and delivery. Stefan's built a routing algorithm that, um, that minimizes pickup costs for both sides. It's a game-changer.

[18:48] Stefan: [nods] Yeah, so most transactions, we're saving 20% to 30% on logistics compared to standalone arrangements.

[18:54] Luca: Right. So we create value beyond just the match. Um, we're raising €150K for 6% of the company. We'll use this to expand from our current market, which is, um, primarily Northern Italy and Milan, to the broader EU. We think this model scales across Europe.

[19:08] Luca: And that's the pitch. Questions?

[19:09] [pause, few hands raise]

[19:10] Ava: Yeah, I've got some. So you mentioned 45 companies. How many active sellers versus buyers? And — more importantly — what's your customer acquisition cost?

[19:19] Luca: Good question. Um, so of the 45, maybe 30 are on the supply side. 15 on the demand side. And CAC is, um, [pause] it's been about €800 per seller. We've been doing in-person meetings, um, one-on-one pitches to construction firms.

[19:33] Ava: So €800 to acquire one seller. And on average, how much revenue does a seller generate?

[19:38] Luca: So the average transaction is, um, around €2,000. And we take 9%. So that's €180 per transaction.

[19:45] Ava: €180 per transaction. And each seller, on average, how many transactions do they do with you in a year?

[19:51] Luca: Um, probably two. Maybe two-and-a-half.

[19:54] Ava: So two transactions per year times €180 take rate is €360 in annual revenue per seller. And you paid €800 to acquire them.

[19:59] Luca: [defensive] But they have multiple buyers...

[20:01] Ava: [interrupting] Wait, let me finish. So you're spending €800 to earn €360 in the first year. When does that break even?

[20:07] Luca: Well, um, it's lifetime value. If they stay with us for three years, it's €1,080 in total take rate. So the payback is around two years.

[20:16] Ava: Two years is a long payback. And that assumes zero churn and no increase in CAC as you move into new markets. In Germany, for example, do you think CAC stays at €800?

[20:26] Luca: [pause, more defensive] It might be higher. But we're also working on, um, organic growth channels. Referrals, word-of-mouth. And we're building out our demand side so that sellers are getting more transaction opportunities.

[20:37] Ava: Okay. But you're not there yet. Right now, it's one-on-one outreach.

[20:41] Luca: Right, currently.

[20:42] Ava: Got it. Next question — your marketplace is geographically limited, right? Pickup and delivery logistics only work within a certain radius?

[20:49] Luca: Um, yeah. Our current model is optimized for, like, a 50km radius. Beyond that, logistics become prohibitive. Most of our transactions are within 25km.

[20:58] Ava: So you're regional, not national.

[21:00] Luca: We're regional now, but we're planning to, um, to build out regional hubs in other European cities.

[21:05] Ava: [skeptical] Regional hubs. So you'd need to replicate your operational model in each new market. That means local teams, local relationships, local pickup infrastructure.

[21:12] Luca: Yeah, that's the plan.

[21:14] Ava: Which is not like a software company. It's more like a, um, like a logistics network.

[21:18] Luca: [quietly] Well, yes and no. The software is highly leveraged. We build the routing algorithm once—

[21:22] Ava: [interrupting] I understand. But your capital intensity is going to be high. You're going to need a team in each market. That's not scalable in the way we usually think about software.

[21:30] [uncomfortable pause in the room, audience energy noticeably shifts]

[21:31] Daniel: Stefan, can I ask — what's your background? Where did you come from?

[21:35] Stefan: I was a COO at a logistics company for three years. Before that, supply chain management at a manufacturing firm.

[21:41] Daniel: And you're comfortable building out a multi-market logistics network?

[21:44] Stefan: I've done it before. The complexity is real, but it's doable.

[21:48] Daniel: [nods slowly but noncommittally] Interesting.

[21:49] Tomás: So I want to, um, I want to circle back to the CAC for a second. You said €800 per seller in Northern Italy. But when you expand to Germany, do you think you can do that organically or — or are you going to need even more in-person effort?

[21:60] Luca: It'll probably take more effort initially. Germany's a bigger market but also more fragmented. We might need to, um, hire local teams to do the intro meetings.

[22:06] Tomás: Right. So CAC could double.

[22:08] Luca: Potentially.

[22:09] Tomás: So your unit economics get worse before they get better.

[22:12] Luca: [pause] In the near term, yes. But we're betting that as we build supply on the demand side, um, referrals and word-of-mouth kick in. And we can shift to a more, um, a more scalable acquisition model.

[22:23] Ava: But you don't have evidence of that yet.

[22:25] Luca: Not yet.

[22:26] [another uncomfortable pause]

[22:27] Daniel: Alright. I think we've got the picture. [glances at Ava, who's looking at her notes] The problem is real. Construction waste is a real problem. But the unit economics and the geographic expansion challenge are significant. We'll need to, um, we'll need to think carefully about whether this is venture-scale.

[22:42] Luca: Sure. I understand.

[22:43] Ava: [looking up] One more quick thing — how much of your €180K GMV is recurring? Like, what's the percentage of repeat sellers?

[22:50] Luca: Um, about 60% of our sellers have done more than one transaction.

[22:54] Ava: 60%. That's decent. But new sellers are only doing 1.4 transactions on average?

[22:59] Luca: [defensive again] We're early. Retention will improve as we expand supply.

[23:02] Ava: Maybe. But right now, repeat transaction rate is low. That's a concern.

[23:07] Luca: Fair point.

[23:08] Daniel: Okay. Thanks for the pitch. We'll follow up. [polite applause]

[23:10] [applause is noticeably quieter than after NovaTech. A few people in the audience are checking their phones. The energy has definitely cooled]

[23:16] Daniel: [whispers back] Yeah. The math doesn't work.
