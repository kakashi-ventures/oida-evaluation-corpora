# Meeting Transcript: Demo Day Alpha - UrbanFlow Pitch
**Date:** November 12, 2025
**Location:** Palazzo delle Industrie, Milan
**Event:** Demo Day Alpha - Fifth and Final Pitch
**Attendees:** Daniel Reeves (CEO, Vertex Minds), Ava Lindström (Co-IC, Vertex Minds), Tomás Herrera (Analyst, Vertex Minds), Diana Rossi (Founder/CEO, UrbanFlow), Karim Haddad (Operations Lead, UrbanFlow)

---

[18:45] **Diana Rossi:** [walks to center of stage with energy, smiles at the crowd] Okay, everyone, last pitch of the night. I promise to make it worth your time.

[Audience chuckles. Diana clicks to her first slide — bold typography with a photo of an electric cargo e-bike loaded with packages, Milan's Duomo in the background]

**Diana:** This is the last-mile delivery problem. You run an e-commerce shop, you're selling, I don't know, coffee, shoes, candles — something people buy online. You've got maybe 50 to 500 orders a day depending on your size. You ship them using standard couriers, and your customer gets their package in... three to five days. Or they don't get it at all, because it gets lost in a warehouse outside the city.

[Diana clicks to next slide: a photo of a congested Milan street with cars, buses, and delivery vans]

**Diana:** Meanwhile, you're spending fifteen to twenty percent of your revenue on last-mile logistics. The courier margins suck. The customer experience is whatever. And the environment is... [pauses] ...not great.

[18:50] **Diana:** So we built UrbanFlow. We started in Bologna in August. We have one hub — Karim runs it, actually. [Karim waves from the side of the stage] We're doing about 2,100 deliveries a month with thirty-five merchant partners. We're charging them two-fifty per package, which is about forty percent cheaper than traditional courier rates. And we're making money.

[Diana clicks to financial slide]

**Diana:** We're at six thousand euros MRR. Gross margin is twenty-eight percent. And honestly, we're just getting started.

[The audience shifts forward slightly. People scribbling notes]

[18:54] **Ava:** [immediately] Beautiful pitch, Diana. Really compelling problem statement. Let's dig into the unit economics because that's where logistics lives or dies. Two-fifty revenue per delivery — walk me through your cost structure.

[Diana nods, clicks to a detailed cost breakdown slide]

**Diana:** Yeah, so, we break it down per delivery. Our average revenue is two-fifty euros. Cost structure: the rider labor is about a euro-twenty, um, on average. That includes benefits, that includes the occasional training or bike maintenance from our end. Hub allocation — so that's rent, utilities, sorting labor, that gets us to about forty cents. Bike maintenance and depreciation, we allocate about fifteen cents per delivery. That gets us to, um... [tallies on her fingers] ...one-eighty per delivery.

[Ava nods, jotting notes]

**Diana:** So twenty-eight percent gross margin, like I said. It's tight, but it's not a death march. And here's the key: that cost structure gets better with density.

[18:58] **Daniel:** What's your path to forty percent margin?

**Diana:** We modeled it pretty carefully. At 3,500 deliveries per month per hub, our route optimization gets tight enough that we can get that down to one-fifty in variable costs. That's the sweet spot. Below that, we're not dense enough. Above that, we need a second hub, which resets things slightly.

[Diana clicks to a chart showing cost per delivery declining as volume increases]

**Diana:** So once we hit that 3,500 threshold, we're looking at forty, forty-two percent margins. We're at 2,100 right now, so we've got runway to prove the model.

[Tomás: "When do you project hitting that?"]

**Diana:** Um, if we onboard, say, eight to ten more merchants and our order volume keeps growing like it has been — we're at about fifteen percent month-over-month growth — I'd say Q2 next year. Six months from now.

[19:02] **Ava:** And you're not looking at price resistance from merchants as you scale? Two-fifty seems pretty aggressive against the big couriers.

**Diana:** We're actually saving them money. The average merchant is paying three-sixty to four-twenty with existing couriers, so two-fifty is attractive. And we offer something they don't: next-day guaranteed delivery within the city, no exceptions. That's a product advantage that justifies the price.

[Ava leans back, satisfied for now]

[19:04] **Daniel:** Diana, let's talk market size. You mentioned this is about urban micro-logistics, which is a growing space, but I want to understand your addressable market. What's your thinking?

**Diana:** [pauses briefly] So, European micro-logistics, urban last-mile, is growing at about twenty-five percent annually. That's based on some public research on the US market adjusted for European density, because honestly, there's not a ton of EU-specific data published. We couldn't find—

[Daniel nods, appreciating the candor]

**Diana:** —but based on what we could find, we modeled that Bologna, Milan, Rome, Turin — those four cities alone represent something like 180 million deliveries a year. If we could capture even two percent of that market, we're at 3.6 million deliveries annually, which at our current unit economics, that's... [looks at Karim, who mouths the number] ...roughly 8.6 million euros in annual revenue.

[19:08] **Daniel:** And you're not assuming we'll hit two percent in year one.

**Diana:** [laughs] No, no. Year one is about proving the model in Bologna and maybe one other city. By year three, we're looking at eight to ten hubs across Italy, maybe a push into southern France or Austria if the capital allows. That's when we get to more substantial market share.

[Tomás leans forward]

[19:10] **Tomás:** You mentioned filing a patent. Can you tell us about that?

**Diana:** Yeah, so we filed a provisional patent application in September for our dynamic routing algorithm. The core innovation is that we can chain deliveries across multiple hubs — so like, if a merchant has orders in both Bologna and Modena, instead of splitting them into two courier runs, our algorithm optimizes a relay pattern where Karim's team in Bologna picks up, consolidates, and hands off to a partner hub in Modena on one efficient route.

[Diana clicks to a diagram showing hub nodes connected by delivery routes with arrows indicating flow]

**Diana:** Single-hub competitors can't do that. They're bound by geography. We're building a network.

[Karim chimes in from the side]

**Karim:** The patent actually covers the optimization of that multi-hub relay, the way we schedule hand-offs to minimize time and cost. It's not something you can just copy if you only operate one hub.

[19:14] **Tomás:** Do you have a filing number, or is this still in provisional status?

**Karim:** It's still provisional. We're um, we didn't assign a specific number yet because the Italian patent office, they have their own timeline. Our lawyer is handling it, and we can share the filing documentation.

[Tomás nods, makes a note]

[19:15] **Ava:** Diana, I want to talk about your team and operational resilience. You're in logistics, which means you're hiring riders, managing physical assets, dealing with weather, theft, accidents. Walk me through how you're managing that complexity at 2,100 deliveries a month.

[Diana glances at Karim, who stands and walks to the center]

**Karim:** So right now, it's lean but intentional. We have four full-time riders. I'm running logistics operations plus merchant relationship management. Diana's CEO, doing fundraising, strategy, business development. We have one part-time person who does inventory and bike maintenance.

[19:19] **Karim:** On resilience: we carry insurance that covers theft and damage. We've had two incidents in three months — one stolen bike, one minor accident. The insurance claim process has been smooth. On the rider side, we're hiring locally, training them on our dispatch system, our safety protocols. We're also building in redundancy for the peak season — right now we're planning to bring on two more riders by January for the holiday spike.

**Ava:** What's your rider turnover?

**Karim:** Three months in, we haven't had any churn. The pay is competitive for Bologna, and most of our riders like the flexibility. But yeah, it's early.

[19:23] **Daniel:** Diana, you reached out to Tomás a few weeks ago about your fundraising round. The initial ask was 250 thousand euros, I believe?

[Diana nods]

**Diana:** Yes, that was our first iteration. We talked with Tomás about our burn rate and the phasing of our expansion, and we actually realized that 180 thousand gives us what we need for this phase — it funds the second hub setup, it funds hiring, and it gives us the runway to hit profitability at the two-hub level.

[19:26] **Daniel:** So you're comfortable going smaller?

**Diana:** We are. It means we're a bit more disciplined with merchant acquisition — we can't do a massive marketing spend. But honestly, we've got a wait list. Merchants in Milan and Rome have been asking us when we're coming. So I think we can fill the second hub without blowing money on customer acquisition.

[Tomás smiles slightly]

[19:28] **Daniel:** And what's the structure you'd be looking at? Equity, SAFE, convertible?

**Diana:** We're open. We like SAFE because it's simpler, but if Vertex wants equity, we can do that conversation.

[Daniel and Ava exchange a glance]

[19:30] **Daniel:** [pauses] One more thing. Competitive landscape. There's a bunch of micro-mobility and last-mile startups in Europe now. What's stopping a big logistics player like [Company X] or [Company Y] from doing exactly what you're doing at scale with their balance sheet?

**Diana:** [sits forward] Two reasons. One, they're not incentivized. Their margin is in volume, not in unit economics optimization. Two, unit economics at scale requires local density and local expertise. We're hiring locally in each city, we know the streets, we know the merchants. A centralized organization can't move as fast as we can. And three — [laughs] sorry, three reasons — we're first mover in this model. Even if they copy us, we'll already be in six cities with a brand and a reputation.

[Karim nods in agreement]

[19:35] **Tomás:** What's your timeline for closing the round?

**Diana:** Ideally, mid-December. We want to be funded before the holidays so we can move on the second hub immediately in January.

[Daniel: "That's ambitious but doable."]

**Diana:** We're motivated. [smiles]

[19:38] **Daniel:** [stands, shakes Diana's and Karim's hands] Diana, Karim, fantastic pitch. Really. And honestly, this is the one I'm most excited about from tonight.

[Ava stands and shakes hands as well]

**Ava:** [quietly, to Diana] The honesty about market research was refreshing, by the way. A lot of founders oversell the TAM.

**Diana:** [nods] I figured you'd rather hear the truth than get sold a dream.

[Audience applauds warmly. More sustained applause than the previous pitches. Several investors are nodding.]

[19:40] **[END OF FORMAL Q&A]**
