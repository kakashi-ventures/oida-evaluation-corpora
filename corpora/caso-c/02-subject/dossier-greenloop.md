## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

GreenLoop operates a B2B marketplace connecting construction sites with excess materials to projects requiring those materials. The platform uses logistics optimization and supply-demand matching to reduce waste and procurement costs. The company is raising €400K for 6% equity at a €6.3M pre-money valuation.

**Investment Ask:** €400,000 (6% equity, pre-money €6.3M)
**Recommendation:** **PASS**

While the environmental thesis is sound and the market opportunity is real, GreenLoop's current traction and unit economics do not justify the proposed valuation. Additionally, co-founder Stefan Berger's background raises unanswered questions. At current GMV and customer acquisition trajectory, we believe the company is pre-product-market-fit and would be better served by bootstrapping or seeking smaller checks from impact investors aligned with circular economy thesis.

## PRODUCT & TECHNOLOGY

### Core Offering

GreenLoop provides a digital marketplace where construction site managers can list excess materials (aggregates, timber, metals, concrete, fixtures) and match them to projects with demand for those materials. The platform includes:

1. **Inventory Matching Engine:** Automated matching of supply and demand listings
2. **Logistics Optimization:** Route optimization for material transport
3. **Transaction Settlement:** Payment processing and escrow (work with established payment provider)

### Technical Stack

Backend built in Python/Django. Marketplace frontend in React. Mobile apps (iOS/Android) in early stage. Simple but functional. No proprietary algorithms or defensible tech moat. The matching engine is basic rule-based logic, not ML-driven. Logistics optimization uses third-party APIs (Google Maps, Mapbox) with minimal custom optimization.

### Maturity

MVP launched Q2 2025. Platform is functional but has UX issues noted during pilot phase. Integration with existing site management tools (common in construction) is minimal and planned for later stages. No API yet.

## MARKET SIZING & OPPORTUNITY

### TAM Estimate

**Theoretical TAM: €8.2B annually**

Derived from:
- Estimated €280B annual construction spend across EU-5
- Estimated 15-20% of materials are discarded or shipped inefficiently
- Potential savings if circular procurement were standard
- Take-rate potential: ~3% of materials value = €8.2B

### Reality Check

The TAM estimate assumes dramatic behavior change (adoption of circular procurement across the industry). Current circular economy adoption in construction is <2%. The addressable market at realistic near-term penetration is much smaller: ~€200M.

### Geographic Focus

Currently operating only in Italy and Germany. Both markets have fragmented construction supply chains, which should support a marketplace model. However, regulatory and procurement differences between regions will require localized GTM.

## TRACTION & MARKET VALIDATION

### Current Metrics (as of Nov 2025)

- **Registered Companies:** 45 (suppliers and buyers combined)
- **Completed Transactions:** 12
- **Cumulative GMV:** €180K (all-time)
- **Current Monthly GMV:** ~€25K (Oct–Nov run rate, volatile)
- **Take Rate:** 8%
- **Current MRR:** ~€2K (highly variable)
- **Customers:** No long-term contracts; all transactional

### Unit Economics

- **CAC:** ~€800-1,200 per company (via direct outreach, partnerships with construction associations)
- **LTV:** Unclear; churn is high and repeat transaction rate is low (~30% of suppliers list more than once)
- **LTV/CAC:** Approximately 1.5x (concerning; break-even or slightly negative when accounting for support costs)

### Customer Acquisition Challenges

- Construction site managers are not accustomed to digital procurement for surplus materials
- Traditional suppliers (material brokers) have entrenched relationships
- Convincing both supply and demand sides (two-sided network effect challenge) is capital-intensive
- Each transaction is relatively small (€5K-50K), making platform fee (8%) high from customer perspective

## COMPETITIVE LANDSCAPE

### Direct Competitors

1. **BuildNet (Germany, private)** — Stefan's former company. Serves similar function but focuses on prefab/modular construction. Estimated €2-3M ARR, investor-backed (€500K seed). More established in German market. Superior product UX from what we can observe.

2. **MateralHub (Netherlands, public)** — Focused on scrap/recycled materials. Different positioning but overlapping customer base. Profitable at modest scale (~€5M ARR estimated). More mature, better integrated with waste management logistics.

3. **ReuseIT (France, startup)** — Circular economy materials marketplace. Pre-revenue as of our last check (3 months ago).

### Indirect Competitors

- Existing material brokers and wholesalers (highly fragmented, mostly offline or paper-based)
- Craigslist and local Facebook groups for small-scale material exchange
- Direct site-to-site negotiations (no platform)

### Competitive Position

GreenLoop is not differentiated on technology or features. The main competitive advantages would be (1) network size, (2) low transaction costs, (3) rapid expansion to new geographies. None of these are currently realized.

## FINANCIAL REVIEW

### Historical Burn & Funding

- **Bootstrapped Funding:** ~€80K from founders and 1 angel (no external investors)
- **Monthly Burn Rate:** ~€12K (5-person team, Milan office)
- **Runway:** ~7 months (if no revenue growth)

### Unit Economics Deep Dive

At current transaction rates, the unit economics are problematic:

- Supplier (materials company) lists excess inventory on platform
- Cost to acquire that supplier: €1,000 (sales/outreach)
- Probability that supplier completes a transaction in first 6 months: ~40%
- Expected MRR per acquired supplier: €15 (assume €200 transaction, 8% take rate, ~2 transactions over 12 months)
- Payback period: 67 months (clearly uneconomic)

The underlying issue is that the category (surplus construction materials) is not inherently high-frequency. Site managers don't have regular surplus inventory; they only list when they happen to have overstock or project delays. This makes the marketplace fundamentally "sparse" and unpredictable.

## RISK ASSESSMENT

### Critical Risks

1. **Unit Economics Do Not Work at Scale:** The core unit economics (LTV/CAC ~1.5x) are below threshold for venture sustainability. This is not a problem with execution—it's a problem with the business model. Even with aggressive growth, the margin structure suggests the company cannot profitably acquire customers at scale.

2. **Two-Sided Marketplace Network Effects Are Weak:** Unlike Uber or Airbnb, where supply and demand are recurring and frequent, construction materials are one-off or occasional. This makes achieving critical mass on both sides extremely difficult. The company will struggle to reach a self-reinforcing equilibrium.

3. **Co-Founder Background Uncertainty:** Stefan's 14-month employment gap and his role at a competitor (BuildNet) is a red flag. If he departed BuildNet due to non-compete litigation, IP disputes, or other legal issues, this could create liability for GreenLoop. The lack of transparency on this point is concerning.

4. **High Churn & Repeat Rate Risk:** Current repeat transaction rate (~30%) suggests most customers use the platform once and don't return. This is inconsistent with a successful marketplace business model, which depends on engaged, repeat users.

5. **Valuation Mismatch:** €6.3M pre-money for €180K cumulative GMV and €2K MRR is extremely high. This assumes dramatic growth and is not justified by current traction. Standard Marketplace benchmarks suggest €20M+ ARR or 40%+ monthly growth would justify a €6M+ valuation. GreenLoop is not on either trajectory.

### Medium Risks

1. **Regulatory Fragmentation:** EU construction regulations, labor standards, and waste disposal rules vary by country. Expansion to new markets requires country-specific legal expertise.

2. **Capital Intensity:** Competing with BuildNet (well-funded) and other entrants will require significant capital for customer acquisition and product development. A €400K raise provides only 3-4 months of runway at current burn, assuming growth doesn't materially improve.

3. **Ecosystem Dependencies:** Success depends on logistics partners, payment processors, and site management software integrations. Building these relationships at small scale is difficult.

---

## VERDICT & REASONING

We recommend **passing** on this investment for the following reasons:

1. **Weak Unit Economics:** The company's path to unit economics profitability is unclear. The marketplace category (low-frequency, sparse supply/demand) does not support efficient customer acquisition at current price points.

2. **High Valuation:** €6.3M pre-money is disconnected from current traction. Even at optimistic growth scenarios (3-4x GMV growth per year), the company would reach €2M ARR in Year 3, which would justify a €12-15M valuation at that point, not today.

3. **Co-Founder Risk:** Stefan Berger's background requires deeper vetting. The employment gap and his role at a competitor raises unresolved questions about non-competes, departures, and potential legal exposure.

4. **Market Skepticism:** Conversations with construction stakeholders suggest resistance to changing procurement patterns for surplus materials. The addressable market may be smaller than projected.

## ALTERNATIVE RECOMMENDATION

If Vertex Minds were interested in this category, we would recommend:

1. **Wait 6 months** for traction data at scale (€500K+ cumulative GMV, 50+ repeat customers) before revisiting
2. **Suggest smaller check** (€50-100K from impact/sustainability investors) rather than this institutional round
3. **Resolve Stefan Berger background** through independent legal verification before any capital is committed
